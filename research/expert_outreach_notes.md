# Expert Discovery: Sponsor Supported Care Validation Interviews

Companion file: `expert_targets.csv` (29 named individuals).

## Methodology

* Sourced entirely from public information: LinkedIn public profile summaries and activity (via web search, not scraping of private data), company press releases, earnings call transcripts, law firm bios, conference (HLTH 2025) speaker listings, and bylined trade press articles (Fierce Pharma, Endpoints News, hit Consultant, The Leverage newsletter).
* Prioritized people who have **publicly and specifically** discussed pricing, deal mechanics, minimum scale, or monetization economics relevant to healthcare sponsorship, not just anyone with a healthcare adjacent title. Examples: GoodRx's Q1/Q4 2026 earnings calls (manufacturer partnership counts, Pharma Direct revenue growth), a named analyst's published CPM figures for OpenEvidence's ad supported model, and a former FTC official who worked the GoodRx Health Breach Notification Rule enforcement action.
* No private contact information (personal emails/phones) was collected. Every URL is a public LinkedIn profile, personal newsletter, or law firm bio page. No one was contacted; this is a discovery list only.
* Excluded generic mid level sales reps and duplicate title holders at the same company once 1 2 strong representatives per organization were identified, to keep the list diverse across companies rather than deep in any one.

## Handoff

### Key Findings
* Real, publicly documented examples of "free to the patient, monetized via a third party" already exist at meaningful scale in adjacent healthcare categories: GoodRx (Pharma Direct, manufacturer sponsored pricing, $151M revenue in 2025, +41% YoY), Forus (~$1B valuation, pharma data monetized, free to providers), and OpenEvidence (ad supported, $70 150 CPM, ~$50M revenue run rate, reportedly the fastest healthcare product ever to reach that scale by some VC estimates). These are stronger reference points for interviews than generic pharma marketing contacts.
* The regulatory line Legion would need to navigate is well precedented and has already produced an FTC enforcement action (GoodRx, Health Breach Notification Rule, for sharing health data with ad platforms without consent), meaning there are named lawyers (Pozza, Lacktman, Hennessy, Reisz, Wagner) who can speak to exactly what tripped that wire and how to avoid it.
* Lotus Health AI (the comparable most directly discussed in Legion's own research) has a named, on the record institutional investor (CRV's Saar Gur) and a named individual backer (Aneesh Chopra) who are reachable and have already spoken publicly about the free/sponsorship model's rationale, a rare opportunity to interview someone who underwrote the exact bet Legion is evaluating.
* Employer funded (not pharma funded) free care is a live, named executive accessible alternative pathway (Transcarent/Glen Tullman, Walmart/Cheryl Pegus) worth comparing against pharma sponsorship as an alternative sponsor type.

### Decision Implications
* This list gives Andrew reachable, specific people to test the two riskiest assumptions in the sponsorship thesis: (1) whether pharma/brand dollars would actually flow to a sponsor supported psychiatry product at meaningful scale and price, and (2) whether the privacy/compliance model can be designed to avoid GoodRx's fate. Both have people on this list positioned to give a real (if partial) answer within a single 30 minute call each.
* No one on this list should be treated as a stand in for Legion's own counsel or diligence, lawyers listed here can describe general regulatory boundaries but cannot substitute for Legion specific legal review given psychiatry's added sensitivity (behavioral health data, substance use records under 42 C.F.R. Part 2 territory) which several of the listed attorneys (Reisz, Hennessy) specifically handle.
