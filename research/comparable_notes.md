# Comparable Business Model Notes: Ad/Sponsorship/Underwriting Monetization in Healthcare

**Prepared for:** Legion Health sponsorship case (internal analysis)
**Scope:** How comparable companies monetize healthcare audiences via ads, sponsorship, or underwriting, with pricing mechanics to support an economic model.
**Note on labeling:** Every claim below is tagged as **[Verified]** (primary source/regulatory filing/press release), **[Company claim]** (stated by the company, not independently audited), **[Third party estimate]** (analyst/press modeling, e.g., Sacra, GetLatka), or **[Opinion/commentary]** (blogs, newsletters, critics). Numbers derived by this analyst (not stated directly in a source) are marked **[DERIVED]** with the calculation shown. This document does not answer the Legion strategic question and invents no Legion data.

## 1. OpenEvidence, physician facing, pharma ad funded AI clinical tool

**What it is:** Free AI powered medical search/clinical decision support chatbot for verified U.S. physicians (NPI verified), monetized by pharmaceutical and medical device advertising displayed alongside/around clinical answers.

### Commercial model
* Free to verified U.S. clinicians; revenue comes from pharma/med device advertisers who buy placement adjacent to clinically relevant queries (e.g., an oncologist asking about chemotherapy sees a contextual ad for a relevant therapy) [Company claim / third party description] (C03, C10).
* Ads are structurally separated from the answer generation system; OpenEvidence states "the information system and the ad display system are fully unconnected" and "advertisements shall not be considered an endorsement" [Company claim] (C03).
* CEO Daniel Nadler has publicly confirmed the company "relies on advertising" and that companies "pay for promotions through video ads on the OpenEvidence app" [Verified, CNBC interview] (C02).
* Emerging second revenue line: enterprise/EHR integrated, non ad supported pricing for health systems (Mount Sinai, Sutter Health, Cedars Sinai), explicitly compared by the CEO to "Anthropic's business model", i.e., moving upmarket to per seat/enterprise contracts as ad supported adoption matures [Verified/Company claim] (C02, C05).
* Also monetizing via CME (continuing medical education) credits (since April 2025) and an API/data partnership with Veeva Systems ("Open Vista") targeting life sciences customers (clinical trial matching, drug discovery insight) [Third party research] (C01).

### Reported financials and scale
* Annualized revenue: ≈$7.9M (2024) → ≈$50M (mid 2025, per one estimate) → **≈$100M+ (per company, as of Jan 2026)** → **≈$150M (2025 full year, per Sacra estimate)**, roughly 1,803% YoY growth per that estimate [Third party estimate for the $150M/1,803% figures (C01); Verified for the "topped $100 million in annualized revenue last year" company statement via CNBC/TechCrunch/Fierce Healthcare (C02, C06, C07)].
* Gross margins reported/estimated at ≈90% [Third party estimate] (C01).
* Valuation trajectory (all Verified via press/Crunchbase reporting of funding rounds): $1B (Feb 2025, Series A, $75M from Sequoia) → $3.5B (July 2025, Series B, $210M) → $6B (Oct 2025, Series C, $200M) → **$12B (Jan 2026, Series D, $250M co led by Thrive Capital and DST)**. Total raised ≈$700M (C01, C02, C06, C08, C09).
* Scale: 40%+ of U.S. physicians use the platform; 650,000, 860,000+ verified clinicians (including NPs/PAs) across 10,000+ hospitals; 18, 20 million clinical consultations/month as of Jan 2026 [Verified/Company claim, mixed sourcing] (C02, C06, C07, C11).

### CPM / ARPU mechanics (the key model input)
* **CPMs of $70 to $1,000+**, versus **$5, 15 CPM** for typical consumer social media [Third party estimate, sourced from Sacra equity research] (C01). A second Sacra linked write up cites **$70, $150+ CPM** as the more typical range with $1,000+ as an outlier/premium placement ceiling (C01).
* **Approximately $124 ARPU** (average revenue per physician user) [Third party estimate, Sacra] (C01). **[DERIVED cross check]:** $150M annualized revenue ÷ ≈650,000, 860,000 clinicians ≈ **$174, $231 per clinician/year** using the higher revenue and lower/higher user counts reported by different sources in the same period, directionally consistent with, but not identical to, the $124 Sacra figure, illustrating how ARPU estimates are sensitive to which user count snapshot is paired with which revenue snapshot. Treat $120, $230 as the plausible physician ARPU band rather than a single number.
* Comparable: **Doximity** (NYSE: DOCS), a longer established physician network platform, generates **$570.4M FY2025 revenue** (Verified, 10 K/earnings release) (C12), of which the majority is subscription style "Marketing Solutions" sold to pharma manufacturers on a **brand by brand subscription basis** (not pure CPM), pharma buys audience segments (specialty, credential, location) and content modules (Newsfeed/Workflow/Peer placements), akin to account based media buys rather than open exchange CPM (Verified, 10 K) (C12). A third party estimate cites Doximity ARPU of **≈$228** (C01) [Third party estimate, not verified against Doximity's own per user disclosure, since Doximity does not publicly report ARPU as a discrete metric].
* UpToDate (Wolters Kluwer) comparator: **≈$500/seat/year** licensing fee to hospitals, a subscription model, not ad supported [Third party estimate] (C01).

### Why premium physician CPMs exist (and why patient CPMs are structurally different)
1. **Prescriber leverage / moment of commercial intent:** Physicians are literally the point of purchase decision for prescription drugs. An ad reaching a doctor at the moment they are evaluating treatment options for a specific condition sits directly upstream of a script, the "conversion" (a prescription) is high value and attributable. Patient ad exposure does not carry equivalent purchase authority: patients cannot prescribe themselves a drug; at most they can ask a doctor about it, an indirect and lossy path to conversion [Opinion/analysis, reasoning synthesized across C01, C10] (Opinion).
2. **Small, provable, high value audience:** ≈1M practicing U.S. physicians vs. ≈330M U.S. consumers. Pharma budgets ($20, 38B/year on HCP directed marketing, per various estimates) are concentrated on a tiny, professionally verified population, so per impression/per user economics are naturally much richer [Third party estimate: $20B annual pharma to HCP marketing spend as of 2019 (C10); $38.5B global pharma marketing budget 2023 with 45% to HCPs (C16)].
3. **Identity verification (NPI) enables precision targeting and compliance:** Physician ads are built around the National Provider Identifier (NPI), a 10 digit credentialed identifier, enabling specialty level and even brand level targeting with an audit trail for compliance (Sunshine Act, FDA promotional rules), infrastructure that does not exist for anonymous patient audiences and that publishers must build deliberately to capture premium pharma CPMs [Third party analysis] (C13).
4. **Regulatory/compliance framework favors professional to professional promotion:** FDA rules on fair balance, approved indication claims, and adverse event reporting apply to all pharma promotion, but the entire apparatus (medical legal review, Sunshine Act disclosure, KOL engagement rules) was built around a physician directed promotional model; DTC (direct to consumer) patient advertising carries additional restrictions (mandated risk disclosures, a shrinking regulatory allowance in some proposals) and political scrutiny that a "sponsor a patient's care" model would likely inherit or exceed [Opinion/analysis; DTC context from C15 and C16].
5. **This does NOT transfer to patient audiences** because: (a) patients are not the purchasing decision maker for prescriptions; (b) patient health data is far more legally sensitive to use for ad targeting (HIPAA where covered, FTC Health Breach Notification Rule and Section 5 otherwise, see GoodRx case below); (c) patient audiences are orders of magnitude larger and less differentiated, commoditizing CPMs toward general consumer/health vertical rates ($20, 45 CPM per Facebook healthcare benchmarks, versus $70, 1,000+ for verified physicians) (C01, C14); and (d) using a patient's own clinical context (e.g., a psychiatric diagnosis) to target them with sponsor messaging raises acute ethical and regulatory exposure that does not exist when advertising to a licensed professional evaluating population level treatment options [Opinion/analysis].

### Critiques and risks (explicitly relevant to any Legion analog)
* Critics (The Geyser, Krafty Librarian) argue ad supported clinical AI has "susceptibility to targeted advertising's bad incentives," citing sponsored/pay to play article placement concerns and lack of transparency in article ranking [Opinion/commentary] (C04, C05).
* OpenEvidence's own materials assert advertiser/answer separation, but the tension between "free, ad funded, clinically authoritative" and "funded by the companies whose products may be recommended" is a structural critique that would apply even more acutely to a patient facing, sponsor supported care model [Opinion/commentary] (C05).

## 2. Patient facing ad supported health models

### 2a. WebMD / Healthline, health media display/sponsorship economics
* WebMD Health Corp. (pre 2017 take private by Internet Brands) reported **$705.0M total FY2016 revenue**, of which **$561.3M (80%) was "advertising and sponsorship" revenue**, split further into **$437.0M "biopharma and medical device"** and **$143.0M "OTC, CPG and other"** [Verified, SEC 8 K / earnings release] (C17, C18).
* WebMD traffic during this period: ≈179.5, 199.0 million unique monthly users across the WebMD Health Network [Verified, earnings release] (C17, C18).
 * **[DERIVED] ARPU proxy:** $561.3M advertising/sponsorship revenue ÷ ≈190M average monthly uniques ≈ **$2.95, $3.13 per user per year** (annual ad revenue / monthly unique user count, a rough proxy since MAU ≠ unique annual users and is not an apples to apples ARPU calculation, shown only to illustrate order of magnitude versus OpenEvidence's ≈$124, 230 physician ARPU, a **≈40, 75x** difference).
* More recent (2025) estimate: WebMD ≈80M MAU, ≈$780M annual revenue, ≈38% market share in online health information [Third party estimate, not an SEC filing since WebMD is now private] (C19).
 * **[DERIVED]:** $780M ÷ 80M MAU ≈ **$9.75/user/year** (same caveat as above, annualized revenue against a monthly active count, illustrative only).
* Healthline Media: reported >$100M revenue in 2018, growing >60% YoY, and overtook WebMD in unique visitors by 2019; monetization mix includes direct pharma/hospital advertising (150 clinicians on staff for content review, ≈32% of the ≈150 direct advertisers prioritize "audience quality"/cost per qualified user as their top KPI), programmatic display, performance/affiliate advertising, and newsletter subscriber relationships [Verified/Company interview] (C20, C21).
* **Mechanic distinction from OpenEvidence:** this is a **general consumer health content audience**, not a credential verified prescriber audience, hence a much lower per user yield despite comparable or larger absolute revenue, because the audience is large, undifferentiated, and not positioned at a moment of prescribing authority.

### 2b. GoodRx, pharma ad + discount card hybrid, and the 2023 FTC enforcement
* **Business model:** Free prescription price comparison/discount coupon platform. Revenue lines (FY2024, Verified 10 K/earnings release, C25, C26):
 * Prescription transactions revenue: **$577.5M** (72.9% of $792.3M total), GoodRx earns a fee each time a consumer uses a GoodRx code at the pharmacy, including on repeat refills without re presenting the code.
 * Subscription revenue: **$86.5M** (10.9%), consumer paid GoodRx Gold type subscriptions.
 * **Pharma manufacturer solutions revenue: $107.2M (13.5%), up 26% YoY**, this is the closest GoodRx analog to "sponsor/pharma funded" revenue: pharma manufacturers pay GoodRx for point of sale discount programs and manufacturer funded savings offers presented to consumers.
 * FY2025 total revenue: **$796.9M** (roughly flat YoY) [Verified, earnings release] (C27).
* Scale: >7 million monthly active consumers (MACs) of prescription related offerings as of late 2024; >55 million total consumers have used GoodRx since 2017 [Verified, 10 K/FTC complaint] (C22, C25).
 * **[DERIVED] ARPU proxy (pharma manufacturer solutions only):** $107.2M ÷ ≈7M MAC ≈ **$15/user/year** attributable to pharma manufacturer funded revenue specifically (not the full ARPU, which including prescription transaction and subscription revenue would be far higher, total revenue ÷ MAC ≈ $792.3M ÷ 7M ≈ **$113/user/year** blended across all revenue lines, but only ≈$15 of that is the "sponsor/pharma paid on consumer's behalf" component analogous to a Legion sponsor support model).
* **2023 FTC enforcement (highly relevant precedent for any Legion patient ad model):**
 * Feb 1, 2023: FTC (via DOJ) brought its **first ever enforcement action under the Health Breach Notification Rule** against GoodRx, alleging it shared sensitive health data (specific medications purchased, health conditions) with Facebook, Google, Criteo, and others for advertising/ad targeting purposes, despite promising it would not, and used that data to build custom ad audiences (e.g., uploading emails/phone numbers of users who bought heart disease/blood pressure medications to Facebook for targeting) [Verified, FTC press release + stipulated order] (C22, C23, C24).
 * Penalty: **$1.5M civil penalty**; permanent injunction barring GoodRx from disclosing "Health Information" to third parties for advertising purposes; required consent regime for other data sharing; 20 year recordkeeping/compliance assessment obligations [Verified, stipulated federal court order] (C23).
 * Contextual/non personalized advertising (ads shown as part of the current on site interaction, without profile building or third party data transfer) remains permitted under the order, this is the regulatory dividing line any Legion ad/sponsorship model would need to respect [Verified, order text] (C23).
 * Direct read through for Legion: **using patient clinical/diagnostic data (e.g., psychiatric condition, medication) to build ad targeting profiles or share with ad platforms is a proven, recently litigated enforcement target**, independent of whether HIPAA applies.

### 2c. Ad supported telehealth / mental health apps, repeated monetization failures
* **Woebot Health:** Shut down its free/lightly monetized consumer facing AI chatbot on June 30, 2025, after ≈8 years, pivoting entirely to B2B (healthcare organizations, employers). Its own retrospective diagnosis: consumer mental health monetization splits into (i) a large free tier that drives word of mouth but no revenue, (ii) a mid tier of $5, 15/month subscribers with high churn, and (iii) high acuity users who need care most but are "economically unviable to serve" via a subscription/consumer model [Opinion/commentary, industry retrospective] (C28).
* **Wysa:** 6 million users across 95 countries; raised $20M Series B (Google, others); free to paid conversion stalled below **2%**; pivoted to B2B/NHS/employer channels which proved slow to close [Third party/opinion analysis] (C29).
* **Real (Ariela Safira):** Raised $18M; audio based "therapy alternative" at **$28/month subscription**; 200,000 downloads; shut down March 2024; founder publicly stated the consumer mental health app market was "fundamentally broken" for investors [Third party/opinion analysis] (C30).
* **Cerebral:** FTC fined **$7.1M+ total** (partial consumer refunds + civil penalty) in April 2024 for sharing personal health information of **3+ million patients** with TikTok, Meta, and Google for advertising purposes, plus deceptive "cancel anytime" practices, a second, mental health specific precedent (after GoodRx) for regulatory risk in ad monetizing behavioral health patient data [Verified, FTC action reporting] (C31).
* **Talkspace / BetterHelp (Teladoc):** Illustrates the broader pattern, DTC consumer subscription mental health telehealth, funded by paid social advertising, is **not a durable model on its own**. Talkspace's DTC era stock lost >90% of value; it pivoted to payer/insurance contracted B2B delivery (6,000 credentialed providers, positive EBITDA, $229M 2025 revenue, +22% YoY, ultimately acquired by UHS for $835M in 2026), moving away from ad driven consumer acquisition entirely. BetterHelp, by contrast, stayed DTC/paid social dependent inside Teladoc and has seen **two years of revenue decline** as paid social acquisition economics broke down (Apple ATT impact cited) [Third party analysis/commentary, not an audited academic source] (C32).
* **Read through for Legion:** No mainstream ad supported (as opposed to subscription or payer funded) mental health consumer app has demonstrated durable unit economics at scale. The pattern across Woebot, Wysa, Real, Cerebral, and BetterHelp is a consistent structural mismatch between (a) the population most in need of free/subsidized behavioral healthcare and (b) a revenue model (ads or thin subscriptions) capable of funding clinical grade delivery.

## 3. Sponsorship / underwriting analogues (non programmatic ad models)

### 3a. Pharma manufacturer Patient Assistance Programs (PAPs), direct free drug underwriting
* Pfizer, Merck, Novartis, GSK, and Amgen each operate Patient Assistance Programs/Foundations providing free medicines to eligible (typically uninsured, low income, ≤300% Federal Poverty Level) patients [Verified, company program pages] (C33, C37).
* Structurally: many are run through **legally separate 501(c)(3) foundations** (e.g., Novartis Patient Assistance Foundation, Amgen Safety Net Foundation, GSK Patient Access Programs Foundation) rather than directly by the manufacturer, to maintain independence from the manufacturer's commercial/marketing arm and reduce anti kickback exposure [Verified] (C35, C36, C37).
* These programs are **product specific charity, not audience monetization**, the manufacturer absorbs the full cost of goods (effectively a COGS write off / tax deductible charitable contribution) in exchange for goodwill, patient access/adherence (a patient who can't afford a drug can't generate a refill), and regulatory/PR risk mitigation. There is no advertiser buys impressions mechanic; it is closer to in kind charitable underwriting than a sponsorship media buy [Opinion/analysis based on program structure] (C33, C37).
* Relevance to Legion: this is the closest real world analog to "a company underwrites free care for a defined patient population," but it is (i) funded by the drug manufacturer whose own product is being dispensed (direct COGS/margin absorption, not third party ad dollars), and (ii) not a model where an unrelated sponsor pays to reach or influence patients, it's closer to a discount/write off than a media/advertising economic model.

### 3b. Employer funded mental health benefits (Lyra Health, Spring Health, Modern Health, Headspace/Ginger), PMPM pricing
* **Pricing structure:** Employer sponsored, per employee per month (PEPM/PMPM) or per eligible employee fee, paid by the employer (not the patient, and not an advertiser) for a defined benefit (therapy, coaching, psychiatry sessions):
 * General EAP style benchmark: **$3, $9 PEPM** [Third party estimate, benefits consulting source] (C41).
 * Lyra Health: reported **individual membership $150, $300/month**; **employer sponsored plans $120, $220/month per covered employee** with employers typically covering 50, 90% of cost; premium/psychiatry inclusive plans **$250, $350/month** [Third party estimate, vendor comparison site, not Lyra's own published rate card, Lyra's actual pricing is customized/non public] (C38, C39, C40).
 * Spring Health: estimated **$20, $40/month less than Lyra** per the same comparison source, i.e., roughly **$100, $180/month** [Third party estimate] (C39, C40).
 * Modern Health: roughly comparable to Lyra, ±$0, $50/month [Third party estimate] (C38).
 * Talkspace (employer channel): estimated **$30, $70/month less than Lyra** [Third party estimate] (C38).
 * Headspace for Work/Headspace Health: estimated **$50, $100/month less than Lyra**, i.e., a lower cost, more preventive/wellness oriented tier [Third party estimate] (C38).
 * **Caveat:** none of these vendors publish authoritative rate cards; all figures above are third party/vendor comparison site estimates, not the companies' own disclosed pricing, and actual enterprise contracts are individually negotiated and often confidential.
* **Outcomes based pricing shift (2025):** Lyra publicly announced a move to **"outcomes based pricing"**, no fixed PEPM fee; employer pays for care delivered, with a bonus to Lyra if benchmarks (access speed, satisfaction, clinical improvement, ROI) are hit, or a credit to the employer if not [Verified, company blog announcement] (C42). Spring Health has discussed a similar shift, contrasting it with the traditional "bundled" fixed PEPM fee model [Verified, company blog] (C43).
* **Mechanic distinction from ad/sponsorship models:** This is **B2B benefits procurement**, not advertising. The employer is the payer/customer; the employee is the beneficiary but not targeted with sponsor messaging; and the vendor is paid a recurring subscription like fee (increasingly tied to outcomes) rather than earning CPM based ad revenue. It is the most financially "proven" (largest, most durable revenue) analog among all the models reviewed, precisely because it does not rely on advertising economics at all.

### 3c. Payer sponsored / foundation funded access (brief notes; PAP overlap)
* NeedyMeds and similar directories aggregate manufacturer PAPs, disease specific foundations, and free/low cost clinics, a nonprofit intermediary model connecting patients to (mostly pharma funded) assistance, itself not monetized via ads [Verified, org description] (C44).
* CMS guidance confirms PAPs operate "outside the Part D benefit" specifically to preserve separateness between manufacturer charity and government drug benefit accounting, a compliance structure relevant to any future Legion sponsor arrangement involving insured patients [Verified, CMS] (C45).

### 3d. Failure modes on the employer/payer underwriting side (survivorship bias correction)

**Why this section exists:** §2c catalogs patient ad failures; this section balances that with employer/payer digital health failures so the "underwriting is proven" claim is not one-sided. Research completed 2026-07-07.

* **Pear Therapeutics, Chapter 11, April 2023 (prescription digital therapeutics; payer reimbursed).** First company to win FDA clearance for a prescription digital therapeutic (reSET/reSET O for substance/opioid use disorder, Somryst for insomnia); went public via SPAC at ≈$1.6B (2021); filed Chapter 11 in April 2023 and laid off >90% of staff. Cause per its own CEO and independent analyses: **payer coverage failure, not product failure**, clinicians prescribed (>11K scripts in Q3 2022), patients engaged, outcomes were demonstrated, but **only ≈50% of written prescriptions were filled and ≈41% of filled scripts were paid** (2022 annual report metrics; coverage density remained insufficient); payers denied coverage despite a CMS HCPCS code existing since 2021/2022 [Verified, Chapter 11 filing, CEO statements, contemporaneous coverage] (C49, C50).
* **Mindstrong Health, shut down March 2023 (VC backed, payer partnered mental health).** Raised ≈$160M (General Catalyst, ARCH, Optum Ventures, 8VC); pivoted from smartphone digital biomarkers to virtual mental health care offered **in network through payer partnerships**; wound down patient services in **Feb, Mar 2023**, laid off ≈128 including the C suite, and sold tech assets to SonderMind [Verified, WARN notice, contemporaneous coverage] (C51, C52).
* **Babylon Health, Chapter 7 liquidation, August 2023 (AI first care; payer/employer/value based contracts).** The most structurally relevant failure for an "AI driven care + third party payer" thesis: AI triage/virtual first care company, SPAC'd at ≈$4.2B, took **capitated value based care contracts** with US payers (incl. Medicaid populations and Centene) in which per capita payments failed to cover actual care costs; lost $221M on $1.1B revenue in 2022; US entities liquidated under Chapter 7 in August 2023 after Centene declined to renew [Verified, bankruptcy filings, contemporaneous coverage] (C53, C54).

**Synthesis (what this changes and doesn't):**
* The employer/payer underwriting mechanic is **not a safe harbor**. Its documented failure modes are: (i) *coverage density risk*, a willing buyer class ≠ actual reimbursement at contract scale (Pear); (ii) *engagement/unit economics risk even with payer distribution* (Mindstrong); (iii) *actuarial risk transfer*, capitated/value based contracts can turn "payer revenue" into underwriting losses when care costs exceed per capita payments (Babylon, directly relevant if Legion ever accepts per patient fixed underwriting for an AI pathway whose true episode cost is unresolved, see model A02b/A04).
* What survives: the **Lyra/Spring Health style employer PEPM benefits procurement** model specifically (fee for benefit, not capitated risk; sold to self insured employers, not adjudicated claim by claim by insurers) remains the most financially proven analog *within* this category, but the claim must now be scoped to that specific contract structure, not to "employer/payer underwriting" generally. Pear's failure in particular shows that "payers show interest" and "payers reliably pay per patient" are different facts (exactly Gate 2's "interest alone is not evidence" standard).

## 5. Audience value comparison: patient vs. physician vs. general consumer

| Dimension | Physician audience (OpenEvidence, Doximity) | Patient audience (WebMD, Healthline, GoodRx, mental health apps) | General consumer audience (baseline) |
| | | | |
| Audience size (US) | ≈1M physicians (smaller with NPs/PAs: ≈860K per OpenEvidence's own broader count) | Tens of millions (health condition specific segments still large) | 330M+ |
| Purchase/prescribing authority | Direct, physician writes the prescription | Indirect, patient can request, cannot prescribe | None specific to health |
| Verifiability of identity | High (NPI based, licensed, credential verified) | Low to moderate (self reported, cookie/device based) | Low |
| Typical CPM | $70, $1,000+ | ≈$20, 45 (health vertical premium over ≈$20 baseline; e.g., Facebook Healthcare ≈$27 44) | ≈$5, 20 |
| Regulatory/data sharing risk in targeting | Lower, professional context, Sunshine Act/FDA promotional framework well established | Higher, HIPAA (if covered entity/BA), FTC Health Breach Notification Rule, state health data laws; GoodRx and Cerebral both fined for exactly this | Standard consumer ad tech risk (GDPR/CCPA level, not health specific) |
| Revenue model maturity/scale proof points | OpenEvidence ($100, 150M ARR in ≈1 year of monetization), Doximity ($570M) | WebMD (≈$560, 780M, legacy, declining), GoodRx pharma solutions line ($107M growing), Healthline (≈$100M+) | Massive but low per user yield (Google/Meta scale economics) |
| Direct comparable to "sponsor pays for a patient's free care" | Not applicable, physicians aren't the care recipient | GoodRx pharma manufacturer solutions line is closest (manufacturer subsidizes patient's out of pocket cost, not an ad impression) | Not applicable |

**Why CPMs and ARPUs differ, synthesized:** the premium in physician monetization is not simply "doctors are a smaller audience", it's that (1) the audience sits at the point of a high value commercial decision (a prescription) that the advertiser can plausibly influence and (in aggregate) measure (script lift), (2) the audience can be identity verified at the individual level (NPI) enabling precision and compliance, and (3) decades of regulatory and Sunshine Act infrastructure make professional to professional pharma promotion a well trodden, legally mapped path. None of these three conditions hold for a patient audience: patients are not the commercial decision maker, their identity/targeting data is far more legally fraught to use (as GoodRx and Cerebral's enforcement actions show), and using a patient's clinical situation to select or target sponsor messaging is a fundamentally different, and more exposed, activity than showing a doctor a relevant treatment option while she is actively researching that condition [Opinion/analysis synthesized from the sources above].

## Key Findings

1. **Physician audience monetization (OpenEvidence, Doximity) is real, fast growing, and well documented**, with CPMs 5, 70x higher than general consumer/patient facing health media, driven by prescriber purchase authority, NPI based identity verification, and an established pharma to HCP regulatory/compliance framework. OpenEvidence reached ≈$100, 150M annualized revenue within roughly one year of turning on ads, on a base of 40%+ of U.S. physicians.
2. **No mainstream ad supported, patient facing consumer health/mental health product has achieved durable, scaled unit economics purely on advertising.** WebMD/Healthline monetize via advertising but at 1, 2 orders of magnitude lower per user yield than OpenEvidence, and largely serve pharma brand awareness/DTC budgets, not a "sponsor funds this specific patient's care" mechanic. Ad supported or thinly subscribed consumer mental health apps (Woebot, Wysa, Real) have repeatedly failed to close their unit economics and pivoted to B2B or shut down.
3. **The closest real world analogs to "a sponsor pays so a patient receives free care" are (a) pharma manufacturer Patient Assistance Programs (a charitable/COGS absorption model, not an ad buy, and specific to the sponsor's own drug) and (b) employer sponsored PEPM mental health benefits (Lyra, Spring Health), which are B2B benefits procurement, not advertising, and are the most financially proven model in this entire review**, precisely because they do not depend on advertising economics or on monetizing patient attention/data.
4. **Using patient clinical/behavioral data to target advertising carries proven, recent regulatory tail risk**, independent of HIPAA coverage: GoodRx ($1.5M penalty, permanent ban on ad data sharing, 20 year compliance obligations, 2023) and Cerebral ($7.1M+ in fines/refunds, 2024) are both direct precedents in exactly the "health/mental health app monetizes patient data via advertising" pattern.
5. **eCPM/ARPU benchmarks span roughly two orders of magnitude** depending on which audience is being monetized: ≈$5, 20 CPM/general consumer, ≈$20, 45 CPM/patient health vertical, ≈$70, 1,000+ CPM/verified physician. Any Legion model assuming patient side sponsor CPMs anywhere near OpenEvidence's physician side numbers would be a significant, currently unsupported assumption.
6. **The employer/payer underwriting side has its own documented 2023 failure cluster**, Pear Therapeutics (Chapter 11 despite FDA clearance and willing prescribers, killed by payer coverage density), Mindstrong (shutdown despite $160M and payer partnerships), and Babylon Health (Chapter 7 after capitated value based contracts under priced actual care costs). "Most financially proven analog" therefore applies specifically to the fee for benefit employer PEPM structure (Lyra/Spring Health), not to payer reimbursed or capitated digital health models generally, see §3d.

## Decision Implications for Legion (input parameters only, no recommendation made here)

* **Plausible sponsor ARPU range per patient**, if modeled on a GoodRx like "manufacturer/sponsor subsidizes a specific benefit" mechanic: order of magnitude **$10, 20/patient/year** for a narrow sponsor line item (GoodRx pharma manufacturer solutions ARPU proxy), versus **$100+/patient/year** only if Legion could achieve a blended, multi revenue stream model similar to GoodRx's *total* company ARPU (≈$113/user/year, derived), which took GoodRx over a decade and multiple product lines (transactions + subscriptions + pharma) to build, not a pure ad play.
* **If modeled on employer/payer sponsored PEPM logic** (arguably the more defensible analog for "sponsor funds free care," since it avoids ad targeting risk entirely): **$3, 9 PEPM at the low/EAP end, $120, 350/month at the premium Lyra/Spring Health end**, i.e., **$36, $4,200/patient/year** depending on richness of benefit, but note this is an *employer paying for its own employees' care*, not a third party brand sponsor paying to reach unrelated patients; the underwriting logic (self interest in workforce health/productivity) does not automatically transfer to a generic "sponsor" in Legion's context.
* **Health eCPM ranges for the model**: use ≈$20, 45 CPM if assuming a general patient health vertical ad placement (Facebook Healthcare benchmark, non targeted or contextually targeted only, to stay inside the FTC's "contextual advertising" safe harbor); do **not** assume physician level CPMs ($70, 1,000+) for a patient audience, that would conflate two structurally different markets per Section 5 above.
* **Contract size / sales cycle for a pharma style sponsorship deal**: pharma marketing agency retainers run $5K, $40K+/month; full pharma CSO/commercial contracts run $500K, $50M depending on scope; enterprise health system/pharma sales cycles typically run 9, 18 months (vs. OpenEvidence's free first strategy explicitly built to bypass this). A Legion sponsorship sales motion aimed at pharma or CPG sponsors should budget for a multi quarter enterprise sales cycle, not a self serve ad exchange motion.
* **Underwriting/PAP style model** avoids ad targeting regulatory risk entirely but requires the sponsor to have a direct product/margin interest in the patient population (as pharma manufacturers do with their own drugs), this logic does not obviously extend to a sponsor with no product being dispensed to the patient.

## Remaining Unknowns

* No public source gives a directly comparable "sponsor pays $X per patient for X free psychiatric care" benchmark; every analog here requires translation/adaptation (physician ad CPM, general consumer health CPM, PAP charitable cost, or employer PEPM), each with different payer identity and different regulatory exposure.
* OpenEvidence's actual per clinician revenue is disputed across sources within the same time window ($124 vs. $174, 231 derived), underlying user counts and revenue snapshots are not perfectly time aligned across secondary sources, and OpenEvidence has not published a first party ARPU figure.
* No public data on GoodRx's contract size or sales cycle length specifically for its pharma manufacturer solutions line (as opposed to CSO/agency benchmarks for pharma marketing generally).
* No public case study exists of a sponsor underwriting free *specialty/psychiatric* care specifically (all patient assistance program precedents found are drug specific, not care/visit specific).
* Actual current (2026) Lyra/Spring Health PEPM rate cards are not public; all figures are third party vendor comparison site estimates and may be stale or inaccurate.
