# Regulatory and Risk Research Notes: Sponsor Supported Care Models in Patient Facing Telehealth Psychiatry

**Prepared by:** Regulatory and Risk Research Agent
**Scope:** Maps the regulatory perimeter for advertising, sponsorship, and pharma partnership models in a patient facing telehealth psychiatry context. This document does **not** answer the business question of whether Legion should invest in sponsor supported free care, it identifies the legal issues that any such model must navigate, distinguishes verified law/regulation from firm commentary, and flags what requires counsel review.
**Accessed:** 2026 07 07

## 1. HIPAA Marketing Rule

### 1.1 What counts as "marketing" (verified, statute/regulation)

Under 45 CFR §164.508(a)(3) and the accompanying HHS guidance, "marketing" is defined as "a communication about a product or service that encourages recipients of the communication to purchase or use the product or service." With very limited exceptions, a covered entity may not use or disclose PHI for marketing **without prior written patient authorization**. [HHS, "Marketing," 45 CFR 164.501/164.508(a)(3)]

Two categories are especially relevant to a sponsorship model:
* **Communications involving third party remuneration**: "An arrangement between a covered entity and any other entity whereby the covered entity discloses PHI to the other entity, in exchange for direct or indirect remuneration, for the other entity ... to make a communication about its own product or service" is marketing with **no exceptions**, authorization is always required, and the authorization must disclose that remuneration is involved. [45 CFR §164.508(a)(3)(ii); HHS Marketing FAQ]
* **Face to face communications and nominal gifts** are the only exceptions that do not require authorization. [HHS FAQ 278]

**Why it matters for sponsorship:** If Legion contemplates any arrangement where a pharma sponsor pays Legion (directly or indirectly) to communicate about the sponsor's product to patients, or where patient PHI is disclosed to the sponsor to enable such communications, this falls squarely in the "no exceptions" category of the marketing definition and requires individual written authorization. Merely offering "free care" funded by a sponsor is not per se marketing, but any patient directed communication that references the sponsor's product likely is.

### 1.2 Online tracking technology guidance and litigation status (verified, enforcement/litigation posture is unsettled)

* **Dec 2022**: OCR issued a bulletin stating that HIPAA obligations are triggered when tracking technologies (pixels, cookies, session replay scripts) on patient portals or even unauthenticated public webpages collect information that constitutes PHI, including combining an individual's IP address with a visit to a webpage addressing specific health conditions (the "Proscribed Combination"). [HHS OCR, "Use of Online Tracking Technologies," Dec. 1, 2022]
* **July 20, 2023**: OCR and FTC sent a joint warning letter to ~130 hospitals, telehealth providers, and health app developers about tracking technology risks, explicitly citing GoodRx and BetterHelp enforcement actions. [Wilson Sonsini, June 25, 2024]
* **March 18, 2024**: OCR issued a Revised Bulletin softening some language but retaining the core rule.
* **June 20, 2024**: N.D. Tex. in *American Hospital Association v. Becerra* vacated the "Proscribed Combination" portion of the guidance, holding OCR exceeded its statutory authority in expanding the definition of "individually identifiable health information" (IIHI). The court declined to permanently enjoin OCR. [*Am. Hosp. Ass'n v. Becerra*, No. 4:23 cv 1110 (N.D. Tex. June 20, 2024)]
* **Aug 29, 2024**: OCR withdrew its appeal to the Fifth Circuit. [Epstein Becker Green, healthlawadvisor.com]
* **Current status (as of research date)**: The vacated portion applies narrowly to the specific IP address + unauthenticated webpage combination. Tracking on **authenticated** patient portals and telehealth platforms remains squarely within HIPAA's scope per undisputed portions of the guidance, user authenticated pages "generally have access to PHI." Firm commentary (Wilson Sonsini, Nixon Peabody) cautions that FTC Act Section 5, the revised Health Breach Notification Rule, and state laws (e.g., Washington My Health My Data Act) continue to apply regardless of the HIPAA litigation outcome. [Nixon Peabody, July 3, 2024, commentary, not binding law]

**Why it matters for sponsorship:** Legion's patient portal and telehealth session pages are authenticated and clinical in nature, tracking pixels there remain clearly within HIPAA's reach notwithstanding the Texas ruling. Any ad tech or analytics pixels used for sponsor attribution/measurement on authenticated pages need a BAA with the vendor and PHI compliant configuration, or need to avoid PHI transmission entirely.

## 2. FTC Enforcement: Health Breach Notification Rule, GoodRx, BetterHelp

### 2.1 Health Breach Notification Rule (HBNR), 2024 Final Rule (verified, final rule, effective July 29, 2024)

The HBNR (16 CFR Part 318) requires vendors of "personal health records" (PHRs) and related entities **not covered by HIPAA** to notify individuals, the FTC, and (for breaches ≥500 people) media of a breach of unsecured, identifiable health data. [FTC, Final Rule, 89 Fed. Reg. 47,028 (May 30, 2024)]

Key clarifications in the 2024 amendments:
* Confirms the Rule covers **health apps and similar direct to consumer technologies**, not just traditional PHR vendors, by adding definitions of "health care provider" and "health care services or supplies" (explicitly including mental health tracking tools).
* Clarifies that a "breach of security" includes **unauthorized disclosures**, not just cybersecurity intrusions, i.e., sharing data with an advertiser without authorization can itself trigger notification obligations.
* Expands notice content requirements (must identify third parties that received the data, describe types of information involved).
* Effective date: July 29, 2024. Violations are treated as FTC Act Section 5 violations, subject to civil penalties. [FTC Business Blog, April 25, 2024]

**Why it matters for sponsorship:** If Legion's app/portal functions as a "personal health record" (drawing information from multiple sources) and is not fully HIPAA covered for some data flows, disclosing patient data to a sponsor for advertising/marketing without authorization could itself be a "breach" requiring notification, independent of any hacking event.

### 2.2 GoodRx enforcement action (verified, first of kind FTC HBNR enforcement, 2023)

* **Feb 2023**: FTC's first ever HBNR enforcement action. Complaint alleged GoodRx violated FTC Act Section 5 and the HBNR by: (1) disclosing PHI (names, addresses, health info) to Facebook, Google, Criteo, and others for advertising despite promising not to; (2) misrepresenting DAA (Digital Advertising Alliance) compliance; (3) falsely implying HIPAA protection; (4) failing to notify users, FTC, and media of the unauthorized disclosure ("breach") as required by HBNR.
* **Penalty**: $1.5 million civil penalty (paid to U.S. Treasury), plus a **novel injunctive remedy**: a flat prohibition on sharing user health data with third parties for advertising purposes, and a requirement to obtain consent before sharing for any other purpose. [FTC v. GoodRx Holdings, Stipulated Order, Case No. 3:23 cv 460 (N.D. Cal.), Feb. 17, 2023; DOJ press release, Feb. 22, 2023]

### 2.3 BetterHelp enforcement action (verified, FTC, 2023; directly analogous to psychiatry/mental health telehealth)

* **March 2023 (proposed) / July 2023 (final)**: FTC alleged BetterHelp (online counseling, including "MyTherapist," "Teen Counseling," "Pride Counseling," etc.) promised to keep health data private but disclosed consumers' email addresses, IP addresses, and **intake questionnaire answers** (e.g., "Have you ever been in therapy before?" and financial status responses) to Facebook, Snapchat, Pinterest, and Criteo for advertising/retargeting purposes, including data on people who never became paying customers.
* **Penalty**: $7.8 million, refunded directly to consumers (first FTC action returning funds to consumers over health data misuse). Final order **bans BetterHelp from sharing health data for advertising**, requires affirmative express consent before sharing personal info with third parties for any purpose, requires deletion instructions to recipients, and mandates a comprehensive privacy program audited biennially for 20 years. [FTC, "FTC to Ban BetterHelp...," March 2, 2023; Final Order, In re BetterHelp, Inc., Docket No. C 4796, July 14, 2023]

**Why it matters for sponsorship:** BetterHelp is the closest factual analog to Legion's business (patient facing mental health telehealth). The enforcement theory did not require a "hack", ordinary ad tech integrations (Meta Pixel, Snap Pixel) sharing intake/questionnaire data were enough to trigger liability. Any model where sponsor related ad tech touches psychiatric intake data, diagnosis signals, or session adjacent data carries direct, precedented FTC exposure, with a demonstrated willingness by FTC to order structural remedies (data sharing bans) beyond fines.

## 3. Anti Kickback Statute, Beneficiary Inducement CMP, and Free Care Issues

### 3.1 Core framework (verified, statute and OIG sub regulatory guidance)

* **Anti Kickback Statute (AKS)**, 42 U.S.C. §1320a 7b(b): criminal, intent based statute prohibiting offering/paying/soliciting/receiving remuneration to induce referrals or purchases of items/services reimbursable by a **federal** health care program (Medicare, Medicaid, TRICARE, CHAMPVA, etc.). Felony; up to 10 years imprisonment, $100,000 fine, program exclusion. [42 U.S.C. §1320a 7b(b); OIG FAQ, March 2023]
* **Beneficiary Inducements CMP**, 42 U.S.C. §1320a 7a(a)(5): civil, administrative statute, narrower in some ways (applies only to Medicare/state health care program beneficiaries, and only to the offeror, not the recipient) but **broadly defines "remuneration"** to include "transfers of items or services for free or for other than fair market value." Penalties up to ~$20,866 per item/service (inflation adjusted) plus treble damages and possible exclusion. [Social Security Act §1128A(a)(5), (i)(6); 42 CFR §1003.110]
* **Critically: fair market value is not a safe harbor.** OIG has repeatedly and explicitly rejected the argument that FMV remuneration cannot violate AKS, "any assessment of liability under the AKS depends on the facts and circumstances ... including the intent of the parties." [OIG FAQ, "General Questions Regarding Certain Fraud and Abuse Authorities," March 22, 2023, citing OIG Compliance Program Guidance for Pharmaceutical Manufacturers, 68 Fed. Reg. 23,731 (2003)]

### 3.2 Free/discounted care and financial need exceptions (verified)

* Neither AKS nor the Beneficiary Inducements CMP generally prohibits providers from giving free or discounted care to **uninsured or underinsured** patients unable to pay, or from **advertising** financial assistance policies for such patients. [OIG FAQ #7 8, March 2023]
* A specific CMP exception protects free/discounted items or services (not cash/cash equivalents) for **financially needy individuals**, if: (1) not offered as part of advertisement/solicitation, (2) not tied to other reimbursable services, (3) reasonably connected to the patient's medical care, and (4) provider makes a good faith financial need determination. [2020 OIG Final Rule, 85 Fed. Reg. 77,684; Davis Wright Tremaine, 2017 summary of prior rule]
* Nominal value gifts: up to $15/item, $75/year aggregate per patient (as of the 2016 update) do not require an exception. Big box or general purpose gift cards are treated as **cash equivalents** and do NOT qualify, even under this nominal exception. [OIG Special Advisory Bulletin, "Offering Gifts and Other Inducements to Beneficiaries"; Hall Render, Jan. 2021]
* The 2020 Regulatory Sprint to Coordinated Care added new safe harbors (care coordination, value based arrangements, patient engagement/support tools) that could potentially cover some care coordination adjacent sponsorship features, but these have specific, narrow conditions. [85 Fed. Reg. 77,684 (Dec. 2, 2020)]

### 3.3 Pharma sponsored patient programs, OIG Advisory Opinion pattern (verified, non binding to Legion, but instructive of OIG's analytical framework)

Multiple favorable OIG Advisory Opinions on pharma sponsored "free" programs (testing, counseling, disease awareness education) share a consistent risk mitigation pattern:
* **AO 21 01** (free drug program): favorable because eligibility for the free drug did not depend on which physician/site administered it, the sole limitation (a certified center) was FDA mandated (REMS), not a function of the arrangement itself.
* **AO 22 16** (gift card for completing online patient education): favorable ($25 gift card, once/year) because the education program made no reference to any specific provider, practitioner, or supplier.
* **AO 24 12** (pharma sponsored genetic testing + disease awareness education, Feb. 2025): favorable despite implicating both AKS and CMP because of: narrow eligibility criteria, no billing to patient/payor for any component, and critically, **no identifiable patient or provider data shared back to the manufacturer** that could enable targeted marketing. OIG explicitly warned the outcome "would likely have been different" with any data sharing back to the sponsor tied to marketing.
* **AO 25 07** (sponsored companion diagnostic test, July 2025): favorable; key safeguards were that the manufacturer's field personnel did not discuss the drug when talking about the sponsored program, and the manufacturer received no data enabling it to identify providers or patients or to target sales/marketing activities.
* Common thread across all favorable opinions: **the presence of any data flow back to the sponsor that could be used for marketing/targeting is the single most consistent factor OIG flags as pushing an arrangement toward an unfavorable outcome.** [Arnall Golden Gregory, Jan. 2025; Goodwin Law, Feb. 26, 2025; Epstein Becker Green, July 22, 2025, firm commentary synthesizing OIG opinions]

**Why it matters for sponsorship:** Advisory opinions are fact specific and legally binding **only to the requesting party**, Legion cannot rely on them directly and would need its own opinion or counsel analysis. But the pattern is instructive: (1) sponsor funded free care for Legion patients would very likely implicate both AKS and the Beneficiary Inducements CMP as "remuneration"; (2) risk is elevated to the extent Legion bills federal health care programs (Medicare/Medicaid) for any wrap around services enabled by the free care; (3) risk is sharply elevated if any patient or provider identifiable data flows back to the pharma sponsor for marketing/targeting purposes; (4) risk is reduced (but not eliminated) if eligibility is based on clinical/financial need criteria unconnected to which provider/plan the patient uses, and if free care is not conditioned on prescribing or recommending the sponsor's product.

## 4. FDA Drug Promotion Rules

### 4.1 Governing framework (verified, statute, regulation, and OPDP practice)

FDA's Office of Prescription Drug Promotion (OPDP), within CDER, reviews prescription drug promotional material (TV/radio ads, written/printed material, internet/social media, speaker programs, sales rep presentations) to ensure it is:
* Not false or misleading;
* **"Fair balance"** between efficacy/benefit and risk information (content and prominence);
* Consistent with FDA approved labeling (no **off label** promotion, promoting a use, population, dosage, or claim not in the approved label); and
* Adequately substantiated. [FDA, "The Bad Ad Program"; 21 CFR §202.1(e)(6) (7)]

A **2023 final rule** (effective 2024, published Nov. 21, 2023) established specific standards for how the "major statement" of risks in DTC TV/radio ads must be presented "clearly, conspicuously, and in a neutral manner" (plain language, comparable audio clarity, concurrent on screen text, no distracting visual/audio elements during the risk disclosure). [FDA Final Rule, 88 Fed. Reg. 76,996, Nov. 21, 2023]

### 4.2 Disease awareness vs. product promotion (verified regulatory distinction; some findings are FDA funded research, not binding rule)

* **"Disease awareness" (unbranded/help seeking) communications** are not subject to FDA drug advertising requirements **if** they do not name or visually/contextually identify a specific product. The moment a communication is "perceptually similar" to, or placed close in time/place to, a branded product piece, FDA may treat the two as a single promotional unit subject to fair balance and off label rules. [King & Spalding CLE deck, citing FDA Compliance Policy Guides, firm commentary synthesizing FDA framework, directionally accurate but not a primary citation]
* FDA funded research (OPDP "Disease Awareness and Prescription Drug Promotion on Television," completed 2024) empirically found that **mere exposure** to a disease awareness ad, even without an explicit product link, caused consumers to conflate the disease ad's implied benefits/risks with a co occurring branded ad; greater similarity/proximity increased this conflation. This is FDA's own research finding, not a legal rule, but it signals the direction OPDP scrutiny is likely to take with "sponsored education" content that sits near branded content. [FDA/OPDP Completed Research Projects, Sept. 2024]

### 4.3 Off label and unsolicited request rules (verified, FDA guidance documents)

* Companies may not proactively promote off label uses but **may respond to genuinely unsolicited questions** about off label use, under detailed conditions distinguishing public vs. private responses (public responses must be limited to referring the requester to medical/scientific contacts; private responses must be balanced, documented, accompanied by full labeling). [FDA Draft Guidance, "Responding to Unsolicited Requests for Off Label Information," Dec. 2011]
* Distribution of off label scientific/medical publications to **healthcare professionals** (not consumers) is permitted under a detailed safe harbor guidance, with required disclosures of study limitations and author conflicts. [FDA Draft Guidance, "Distributing Scientific and Medical Publications on Unapproved New Uses," 2014]

**Why it matters for sponsorship:** If a pharma sponsor funds "educational" content for Legion's patients about a psychiatric condition (e.g., depression, ADHD) that sits near or links to branded content, FDA's own research suggests real risk of "benefit/risk conflation" that OPDP scrutinizes. Any content strategy needs a clear firewall (in timing, branding, hosting, and staff role separation) between unbranded disease education and product specific promotion, and Legion clinicians/reps must not use sponsor provided talking points that stray into indications, dosing, or safety claims beyond FDA approved labeling.

## 5. State Specific Issues: Corporate Practice of Medicine, State Health Data Laws, 42 CFR Part 2

### 5.1 Corporate Practice of Medicine (CPOM) (verified as to general doctrine; state by state classification below is firm commentary, not primary law, varies by state and changes frequently)

* CPOM doctrine, in states that enforce it, prohibits non physician owned entities from employing physicians or exercising control over clinical decision making; enforcement mechanisms and strictness vary widely by state. [General doctrine verified across multiple firm sources; specific state classifications are secondary commentary]
* One firm source classifies ~16 states as "strict" (only PC/PLLC exception): California, Colorado, Connecticut, Delaware, DC, Massachusetts, Michigan, Montana, Nevada, New Hampshire, North Carolina, Oregon, Pennsylvania, West Virginia, Wisconsin, Wyoming. ~17 "moderate," ~18 "lenient." **This state count/classification is a law firm's (Permit Health / NSU Law Review) secondary synthesis and should be verified by counsel for Legion's specific operating states before reliance.** [Permit Health 50 State Guide; NSU Law Review, 2025]
* Most telehealth/digital health companies use a "Friendly PC" model (physician owned professional corporation contracts with a management services organization). Enforcement risk centers on the degree of control the MSO exercises over clinical decisions, billing, and staffing.
* **Oregon SB 951 (signed June 9, 2025)** is a notable recent tightening: restricts MSO ownership/control over PCs, prohibits MSOs from having ultimate authority over clinical staffing, diagnostic coding, or billing/collection policy, a template other states may follow. Effective dates are staggered (Jan. 1, 2026 for new entities; Jan. 1, 2029 for pre existing ones). [Foley & Lardner, June 17, 2025]

**Why it matters for sponsorship:** If a sponsorship arrangement gives the sponsor (or an MSO structure funded by sponsor economics) any influence over which patients get free care, which providers deliver it, or clinical protocols, this compounds CPOM risk in strict states, independent of AKS exposure. This is a distinct legal question from healthcare fraud/abuse law and needs state by state counsel review for every state Legion operates in.

### 5.2 State consumer health data privacy laws (verified, statutory text and effective dates; application to specific Legion data flows requires legal analysis)

* **Washington My Health My Data Act (MHMDA)**, RCW 19.373, effective March 31, 2024 (small businesses June 30, 2024):
 * Applies broadly to any entity doing business in or targeting Washington consumers, **regardless of HIPAA covered status** for non PHI data flows, the exemption is field by field, not entity wide.
 * Requires **opt in, affirmative, specific consent** before collecting/sharing "consumer health data" (broadly defined to include inferred physical/mental health status, and data linked to persistent identifiers like cookies/device IDs).
 * Separate written authorization required before **selling** health data.
 * Prohibits **geofencing** around in person healthcare facilities to track, identify, or send ads to people seeking care there.
 * Consumers have deletion rights enforceable against the entity and downstream processors within 45 days. [RCW 19.373; Mintz, May 24, 2023; Goodwin Law, March 28, 2024]
 * Firm commentary (Terms.Law) notes marketing pixels on public "find a therapist" pages and pre provider intake/matching flows are common areas where HIPAA does NOT apply but MHMDA does, directly relevant to Legion's patient acquisition funnel. [Terms.Law, secondary commentary, useful framework but not binding legal guidance for Legion]
* Other state comprehensive privacy laws (California CPRA, Virginia VCDPA, Colorado CPA) similarly classify health/mental health data as "sensitive," generally requiring opt in consent for processing and offering opt out rights for targeted advertising, but these generally **exempt HIPAA regulated PHI**, creating a bifurcated compliance obligation for hybrid clinical/consumer facing products. [Censinet analysis, secondary commentary synthesizing multiple state laws]
* **Montana** recently (HB 397, effective Oct. 1, 2025) extended its Uniform Health Care Information Act specifically to "mental health digital service" platforms, an example of state law explicitly targeting mental health specific digital services, a category Legion falls into. [Orrick, June 2025]

### 5.3 42 CFR Part 2, Substance Use Disorder Records (verified, statute/regulation; applicability to Legion depends on facts)

* Part 2 (42 U.S.C. §290dd 2; 42 CFR Part 2) imposes heightened confidentiality on records from "federally assisted" programs providing SUD diagnosis, treatment, or referral. A **2024 final rule** (effective) aligned many Part 2 provisions with HIPAA (e.g., single "TPO" consent for treatment/payment/operations, once obtained, allows redisclosure per HIPAA rules) but preserved a strict rule: Part 2 records **cannot be used against a patient in legal proceedings** without consent or court order, and this restriction survives even after HIPAA permitted redisclosure. [HHS, "Fact Sheet 42 CFR Part 2 Final Rule," Feb. 7, 2024; 89 Fed. Reg. 12,472 (Feb. 16, 2024)]
* Applicability threshold: turns on whether Legion (or a specific program/service line) is "federally assisted" (e.g., receives federal funding, is Medicare/Medicaid certified, or has appropriate federal tax treatment) AND provides SUD specific diagnosis/treatment/referral. **This is a fact specific applicability question requiring counsel review**, general psychiatric care without SUD specific services may or may not trigger Part 2 depending on program structure.

**Why it matters for sponsorship:** If Legion's psychiatric services include SUD treatment components, sponsor related data sharing (even for outcomes measurement or marketing analytics) would need to separately clear Part 2's consent regime, which is more restrictive than HIPAA and imposes distinct redisclosure prohibitions.

## 6. Consent and Disclosure Considerations for Sponsor Supported Care

The following are drawn from analogous frameworks (HHS OHRP conflict of interest guidance for research, and general medical ethics literature on undisclosed payments), these are **not** directly binding rules for a commercial sponsorship of care model, but represent the closest existing regulatory/ethical analogues and the kinds of disclosure practices counsel is likely to require:

* **HHS OHRP guidance on financial conflicts of interest in research** (a research context analogue, not directly applicable to clinical sponsorship, but instructive) recommends: disclosing the source of funding and financial arrangements to subjects/patients as part of consent; considering whether a party without a conflicting interest should be involved in the consent conversation; and structural options like separating financial decision making from clinical decision making. [HHS OHRP, "Financial Conflict of Interest" guidance, 2004, verified guidance document, but written for human subjects research, not commercial care]
* **Medical ethics literature** (BMJ, AMA Journal of Ethics) on undisclosed sponsor payments to physicians in clinical trials documents patient expectation that they be told when a doctor/program receives payment tied to their care choices, and frames non disclosure as ethically problematic even where not independently illegal. [PMC1123551; AMA Journal of Ethics, Dec. 2015, secondary/ethics commentary, not law]

**Open question for counsel:** There is no single federal "sponsorship disclosure" statute directly on point for a commercial (non research) sponsor funded free care telehealth model. Counsel will need to determine what disclosure Legion must make to patients (e.g., in intake/consent flows) about: (a) that care is sponsor funded, (b) the sponsor's identity, (c) whether the sponsor receives any data or has any influence over clinical protocols, and (d) whether this disclosure needs to be part of a HIPAA compliant authorization (per Section 1) versus a separate informed consent style disclosure.

## 8. Questions Legion's Counsel Must Answer Before Any Live Pilot

1. **Structure & entity**: Will the sponsor relationship run through Legion's clinical PC/PLLC, a separate MSO, or a distinct nonprofit/foundation vehicle? Does the chosen structure avoid CPOM issues in every state where the pilot will run?
2. **AKS/CMP exposure**: Does the sponsor funded free care touch any service reimbursable by Medicare, Medicaid, TRICARE, or another federal health care program? If yes, does the arrangement fit within an existing AKS safe harbor or CMP exception, or should Legion seek its own OIG Advisory Opinion before launch?
3. **Prescribing linkage**: Is eligibility for free/subsidized care in any way conditioned on, or correlated with, prescribing or recommending the sponsor's product? How will Legion document and audit that it is not?
4. **Data flow to sponsor**: What data, if any, flows back to the sponsor (aggregate, de identified, or otherwise)? Does the sponsor's field/sales team have access to any program data that could be used for targeted marketing? (This is the single factor most consistently determinative in OIG's advisory opinions.)
5. **HIPAA authorization design**: Does any planned patient communication about the sponsor or its product meet the definition of "marketing" under 45 CFR 164.508(a)(3)? If so, what does the compliant authorization process look like, and how is it integrated into onboarding without becoming coercive (i.e., is authorization truly optional and not a condition of receiving care)?
6. **Tracking technology audit**: What pixels/SDKs currently run on authenticated vs. unauthenticated pages? Does Legion need BAAs with any ad tech vendors, or should sponsor attribution analytics be re architected to avoid any PHI/authenticated page contact, consistent with the FTC's GoodRx/BetterHelp enforcement theory?
7. **FTC HBNR classification**: Does Legion's app/portal meet the definition of a "personal health record" under the 2024 HBNR? What is Legion's breach notification protocol if a sponsor related data disclosure is later deemed unauthorized?
8. **State law scope**: Which states will the pilot operate in, and what is the applicable CPOM classification and consumer health data law regime (MHMDA, CPRA, VCDPA, CPA, MT mental health law, others) for each? Is a 50 state (or pilot state specific) compliance matrix needed before launch?
9. **42 CFR Part 2 applicability**: Does Legion's service scope include SUD diagnosis, treatment, or referral in a way that could trigger Part 2, given "federally assisted" program criteria?
10. **FDA promotional firewall**: If any sponsor funded content resembles disease awareness or educational material, what firewall (branding, staffing, hosting, timing separation from branded content) is needed to avoid OPDP scrutiny, and who reviews/approves this content before publication?
11. **Patient disclosure & consent design**: What must be disclosed to patients about the sponsorship relationship, and at what point in the patient journey, to satisfy both legal minimums and a defensible ethical standard? Should disclosure be a standalone consent step separate from HIPAA authorization?
12. **Advisory opinion strategy**: Given that all favorable OIG opinions reviewed here are fact specific to the requesting party, should Legion request its own OIG Advisory Opinion (and/or FDA pre review of any co branded educational content) before a live pilot, and what is the realistic timeline for such a request relative to Legion's 6 month window?

## Key Findings

1. **Every major regulatory regime reviewed (HIPAA, FTC, AKS/CMP, FDA, and multiple state statutes) treats "sharing patient/psychiatric data with a commercial sponsor for marketing or targeting purposes" as the highest risk single activity.** This is the most consistent theme across OCR guidance, the GoodRx/BetterHelp enforcement actions, and every favorable OIG advisory opinion (which uniformly hinge on the *absence* of sponsor data access).
2. **BetterHelp is the single closest enforcement precedent to Legion's business model**, an online mental health counseling platform penalized $7.8M and permanently banned from sharing health data for advertising, based on ordinary ad tech (Meta/Snap pixels) sharing intake questionnaire responses, not a security breach.
3. **Free/sponsor funded care is not per se illegal**, but "free" as remuneration triggers AKS and CMP analysis whenever any patient touching the arrangement could have items/services reimbursed by a federal health care program, fair market value is explicitly NOT a defense per OIG.
4. **State law has moved faster and broader than federal law in health data privacy** (Washington MHMDA, Montana's mental health specific law, CPRA/VCDPA/CPA) and several of these laws apply specifically to the pre clinical marketing funnel (landing pages, intake flows) where HIPAA does not reach, this is a distinct compliance workstream from HIPAA/AKS.
5. **The regulatory landscape is genuinely unsettled in places** (HIPAA online tracking guidance partially vacated with appeal withdrawn but no replacement guidance yet issued; CPOM law actively tightening in some states, e.g., Oregon 2025), meaning legal risk assessment done today should be revisited before a pilot launches given the six month horizon.

## Decision Implications for Legion (Regulatory Risk Only Framing, Not a Recommendation)

Without making the underlying business/strategic call (outside this agent's scope), the regulatory research suggests a rough risk gradient across monetization model archetypes, for counsel to weigh:

* **Likely lower regulatory risk**: Sponsor funds general disease awareness content clearly firewalled from any specific product, with zero patient or provider identifiable data returned to the sponsor, and free care eligibility based purely on clinical/financial need criteria unconnected to prescribing choices, this pattern most closely mirrors OIG's favorable advisory opinions and avoids the BetterHelp/GoodRx fact pattern (no ad tech sharing of psychiatric data).
* **Likely higher regulatory risk**: Any model where sponsor payment is linked (even indirectly) to prescribing rates, where patient intake/diagnosis data flows to the sponsor or its ad platforms for targeting, or where free care is marketed using patient PHI without HIPAA compliant authorization, this pattern directly overlaps the enforcement theories in GoodRx, BetterHelp, and would likely fail OIG's advisory opinion risk factors.
* **Genuinely unresolved / needs its own answer**: Anything involving cross state pilots in strict CPOM states (needs jurisdiction by jurisdiction legal design) and anything touching SUD adjacent services (needs a Part 2 applicability determination).

This gradient is offered strictly as a map of *where the legal risk concentrates*, not as a recommendation on business strategy, deal structure, or go/no go.

* Legion's federal health care program participation (does Legion bill Medicare/Medicaid?) was not confirmed, this is a threshold question for AKS/CMP applicability.
