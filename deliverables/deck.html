LEGIONHEALTH Scope and care model02 / 09

The analysis focuses on a proposed free care pathway for uninsured patients

The assignment asks whether patient facing advertising or sponsorship could support free care without distracting from Legion's core reimbursed business. The model below defines what is being evaluated.

Population and starting point

  • Proposed population: uninsured adults needing psychiatric care
  • Legion today: ~3,000 insured actives; 0 uninsured actives (current operations data)
  • Illustrative national pool: ~5.9M uninsured adults with mental illness (Census/NSDUH 2024, sources P01 to P08)

Care pathway modeled

  • Base case: mostly AI led continuity (~$8/episode, internal estimate)
  • Higher cost paths: AI supported sync (~$37/visit) or clinician led care ($538+ /patient/yr)
  • Break even target: $79 to $167 annual care delivery cost per patient at 25% margin (AI pathway)

What is in scope

Third party funding for incremental uninsured free care, including advertising, brand sponsorship, employer benefit fees, and foundation grants.

What is out of scope

  • Replacing Legion's existing insurance reimbursed fee for service model
  • Using $153/visit reimbursement as the cost benchmark for uninsured care
  • Assuming sponsor revenue is validated before buyer and patient testing
Implication: Economics compare funder revenue to delivery cost, not insurance reimbursement. Results apply only if Legion chooses to build a separate uninsured pipeline.

LEGIONHEALTH Market evidence04 / 09

Comparable evidence supports caution, not rejection

Current evidence suggests advertising and sponsorship are unlikely to be primary funders, but employer models and limited sponsorship may still warrant testing under strict compliance constraints. Full source log: research/sources.csv (134 logged sources). Methodology: deliverables/methodology.md.

Lotus Health AI

Patient sponsorship has limited evidence of scaling

Lotus has discussed sponsorship publicly, but public evidence of meaningful sponsor revenue remains limited. The clearest disclosed signal is employer interest at roughly $50/employee/month, which resembles underwriting more than patient advertising.

Source: lotus_notes.md, public press and interview references
OpenEvidence

Physician advertising economics are not directly transferable

Physician audiences command higher ad value because they prescribe and are identity verified. Patient audiences are modeled at $20 to $45 CPM, which supports only low per patient revenue under base engagement assumptions.

Source: comparable_notes.md, OpenEvidence and health media benchmarks
FTC / OIG

Patient data use creates material compliance and trust constraints

FTC actions against BetterHelp ($7.8M), GoodRx ($1.5M), and Cerebral ($7.1M) show enforcement risk when health adjacent data supports advertising. Favorable OIG opinions consistently require no identifiable patient data flowing to funders.

Source: regulatory_notes.md, FTC orders and OIG advisory opinions
Employer models

Employer funding can work, but model design matters

Pear, Mindstrong, and Babylon failed under capitated or underpriced risk models (2023). Lyra and Spring Health illustrate a different approach: per employee benefit fees without unmeasured clinical risk.

Source: comparable_notes.md, company filings and shutdown reporting

LEGIONHEALTH Risks and assumptions07 / 09

Key risks and unresolved assumptions could change the recommendation

The recommendation depends on assumptions that remain untested. Trust spillover to Legion's 3,000 insured actives is a material risk not fully captured in sponsor tier economics alone.

Material risks

  • Core book contagion: 2% churn among insured actives could cost ~$49K to $122K/yr in core revenue, which may exceed brand sponsorship upside at 15K uninsured patients
  • Employer pricing risk: Modeled $330/yr depends on unvalidated PEPM and 55% utilization
  • Pathway mix risk: Higher acuity could push delivery cost above $538/yr, reducing viable funders
  • Compliance risk: Patient data sharing, clinical influence, or prescription linked economics could create legal exposure
  • Procurement risk: Thirty day interviews may show interest without enterprise contract readiness

Unresolved assumptions requiring validation

  • A15: Employer PEPM willingness to pay
  • A21: Benefit utilization rate
  • A05: Sponsor willingness to pay per patient
  • A10/A11: Patient trust and continuation impact
  • A26: Core book trust spillover rate
  • A02b/A04: AI episode cost and frequency at scale
  • A13: Legion reachable uninsured capture rate

LEGIONHEALTH Recommended test design08 / 09

A limited two sided test can validate willingness to pay and patient trust before scale

Estimated budget: $10K to $25K. Legion should not acquire uninsured patients at scale until buyer pricing and patient acceptance are tested.

Buyer side: sponsor and employer willingness to pay

15 to 20 structured interviews
  • Test sponsor, employer, and foundation pricing for uninsured access packages
  • Use Van Westendorp pricing bands at 1,000 patient scale
  • Probe utilization and narrow benefit willingness separately from headline PEPM
  • Decision threshold: median sponsor WTP above $100/patient/yr may justify co funding further design

Patient side: trust and continuation intent

Randomized concept test, 300+ respondents
  • Compare no sponsor vs three sponsor funded framings
  • Proceed if: trust decline ≤0.25 points and continuation intent decline ≤5 percentage points
  • Measure comprehension and sponsor category sensitivity (pharma vs employer vs community)
  • Advertising pilot deprioritized given low modeled revenue and compliance burden
Survey prototypelegion-take-home-assignment.vercel.app (patient trust instrument built; fielding pending)
Output: Evidence on whether any funder will pay enough, and whether patients will accept sponsor presence, before Legion commits to pipeline build or major commercial hiring.
LEGIONHEALTH Thirty day execution plan09 / 09

A thirty day plan should produce an evidence based proceed, modify, or stop decision

Legion operating data is integrated. The next four weeks focus on reducing legal risk, validating demand, and defining a limited pilot only if thresholds are met.

WEEK 1

Align and reduce risk

Objective: Confirm economics, scope, and legal constraints

  • Validate care cost assumptions against internal cost estimates
  • Define target uninsured patient pathway and test boundaries
  • Complete initial counsel review on AKS, CPOM, and data use

Output: Approved test boundaries and updated model

WEEK 2

Validate demand

Objective: Test buyer and patient interest

  • Conduct sponsor and employer interviews (29 target list prepared)
  • Field patient trust survey with randomized arms
  • Identify viable funding structures and pricing bands

Output: Willingness to pay evidence and patient acceptance results

WEEK 3

Design a limited pilot

Objective: Prepare only if Week 2 thresholds look viable

  • Select one funding model for pilot design
  • Define patient experience, measurement, and compliance requirements
  • Build partner shortlist, budget, and success metrics

Output: Pilot plan, budget, and success metrics

WEEK 4

Evaluate and decide

Objective: Make an evidence based recommendation

  • Update model with interview and survey results
  • Compare outcomes with pre registered thresholds
  • Recommend proceed, modify, or stop

Output: Final recommendation and next phase plan

ProceedBuyer WTP meets minimum threshold, patient trust remains within acceptable range, and legal review identifies a viable structure.
ModifyBuyer interest exists, but pricing, patient acceptance, or operating design requires changes before pilot.
StopWillingness to pay is insufficient, patient trust declines materially, or legal constraints make the model impractical.
This week: Authorize counsel review, approve buyer outreach, and field the patient survey. This determines whether a co funder layer is worth piloting, not whether Legion should abandon its core insurance growth path.
